About

I am a Ph.D. candidate in Accounting at the University of Illinois Chicago. My research is broadly motivated by the problem of economic agents reneging on promises‚Äîwhether in financial contracts, supply-chain relationships, or firms‚Äô commitments to payouts. I study how accounting information helps mitigate these frictions by enhancing credibility, facilitating contract design, and sustaining cooperation among parties with conflicting incentives.

My Job Market Paper ("Operational Risk and Financial Covenants") is the cornerstone of this agenda, examining how borrowers' operational risks‚Äîboth demand-side (customer concentration and demand volatility) and supply-side (supplier dependence and fragility)‚Äîshape debt contract design and capital allocation. My broader research interests include financial contracting, supply-chain and operational risk, and AI-based textual analysis of corporate disclosures.

Research

I study how accounting information alleviates contracting and information frictions that arise when economic agents may renege on promises‚Äîacross financial contracting, supply-chain relationships, and corporate payout decisions. My work focuses on how disclosure and performance signals foster cooperation, credibility, and efficient capital allocation.

This agenda spans financial contracting, supply-chain, and operational risk, and AI-based textual analysis of corporate disclosures to extract new economic insights from large-scale data.

Working Papers

  • Operational Risk and Financial Covenants ‚Äî Job Market Paper / Dissertation (2025)
    Examines how borrowers‚Äô demand-side (customer concentration and demand volatility) and supply-side (supplier dependence and fragility) operational risks shape the design of debt covenants. Shows that demand-side risks are associated with performance covenants, while supply-side risks are associated with capital covenants, and that the association between supply-side risks and capital covenants strengthens under the Volcker Rule.

Teaching Methodology and Innovation

I continuously experiment with ways to make accounting intuitive, relevant, and memorable.

In my previous course, I designed an optional Financial Statement Replication Project that allowed students to reconstruct a real firm‚Äôs financial statements from raw disclosures and assess how accounting choices influence reported outcomes. This exercise encouraged independent thinking and helped students see how accounting information translates into economic insight.

Looking ahead, I plan to develop a Punk Rock Accounting project‚Äîa creative framework for reimagining the tone of the classroom. Accounting, much like punk rock, is exciting, passionate, and sometimes misunderstood as noisy or old-fashioned. Yet its enduring value lies in authenticity, transparency, and hope for a better future. By opening class sessions with punk rock music, I aim to set an atmosphere that captures this spirit: structured but energetic, technical yet expressive, and grounded in a belief that accounting‚Äîlike punk‚Äîcan still inspire change.
